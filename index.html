LunaLite: Understanding Light Pollution

Artificial light can wash out the night sky, affect wildlife, and even disrupt our sleep. Explore what causes light pollution and how simple choices can bring back the stars.

How to help Learn more

Quick facts

  • Shielded lights reduce skyglow and glare.
  • Warm lights (≤ 3000K) are friendlier for people and wildlife.
  • Turning off unused lights saves energy and the night.

What is light pollution?

Light pollution happens when excessive or poorly designed artificial light brightens the night sky. Streetlights, billboards, homes, cars, and buildings can all contribute when light shines upward or sideways instead of only where it is needed. This makes it harder to see stars and can disturb natural day–night rhythms.

There are different forms: skyglow (the bright dome over cities), glare (uncomfortable brightness), light trespass (light spilling into places it shouldn’t, like a bedroom window), and clutter (too many bright sources in one area).

Why it happens

Common causes

Unshielded fixtures

Light aimed the wrong way

When fixtures send light upward or sideways, it bounces in the atmosphere and brightens the sky. Using full cut‑off (shielded) fixtures directs light to the ground.

Too bright

Overlighting

Brighter isn’t always safer. Oversized bulbs create glare and waste energy. Choose the lowest brightness that achieves the task.

Blue‑heavy light

Cool color temperature

Cool (blue‑rich) LEDs scatter more in the atmosphere and suppress melatonin more at night. Prefer warm‑white (≤3000K) lighting outdoors.

Why it matters

Impacts on people & nature

Humans

  • Disrupted sleep and circadian rhythms
  • Reduced night vision due to glare
  • Energy waste and higher bills

Wildlife

  • Confused navigation for birds, turtles, & insects
  • Altered feeding & migration patterns
  • Ecosystem imbalance from reduced darkness
Be a night‑sky hero

Simple ways to help

1. Aim it

Use shielded fixtures so light points down. Avoid exposed bulbs that shine sideways.

2. Dim it

Choose lower lumen bulbs and add dimmers or timers. Motion sensors keep areas safe while lights stay off most of the time.

3. Warm it

Pick warm‑white bulbs (≤3000K). Indoors at night, switch to warm lamps to protect sleep.

Quick answers

FAQ

Are LEDs good or bad?

LEDs are efficient, but choose warm‑white options and proper shielding. It’s about how we use them.

Does brighter lighting always mean safer streets?

Not necessarily. Glare from over‑bright lights can make it harder to see. Even, well‑aimed lighting improves visibility.

What’s the easiest thing I can do tonight?

Turn off unnecessary outdoor lights and close curtains. If you have outdoor fixtures, angle them down.
